Introduction

Coral reefs are critical marine ecosystems that support biodiversity and act as natural coastal barriers. However, these ecosystems are under threat due to global warming, pollution, and human activities, leading to significant degradation. Monitoring coral health is essential for conservation and restoration efforts. Traditional monitoring techniques are time-consuming and labor-intensive, making automated solutions highly desirable. This project uses deep learning to classify coral health into three categories: Healthy, Damaged, and Bleached. A pre-trained ResNet50 model is fine-tuned for this task, leveraging transfer learning to maximize performance on limited data. Additionally, Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM) is employed to provide interpretability by highlighting the regions of the images most influential in the model’s decisions.

Data

The dataset, obtained from Roboflow, contains labeled images of corals across the three health categories. It is divided into training and validation sets to assess model performance. The images are preprocessed using transformations to improve generalization and robustness. For training, transformations include resizing to 224 \times 224, random horizontal flipping, color jittering, and normalization using ImageNet mean and standard deviation. Validation data undergoes resizing and normalization to ensure consistency during evaluation.

The dataset exhibits class imbalance, with some coral health categories being underrepresented. To address this, augmentations such as brightness adjustments and random blurs were applied to the training data. A bar chart of class distribution visually confirmed the imbalance, emphasizing the need for these augmentations to ensure equitable learning across categories. The training set is further visualized to confirm the diversity and correctness of the data.

Modelling

This project utilizes the ResNet50 model, pre-trained on ImageNet, to benefit from transfer learning. The model’s final fully connected layer is replaced with a linear layer that outputs predictions for the three coral health classes. To prevent overfitting, a dropout layer with a 50% dropout rate is added before the fully connected layer. The model is optimized using the AdamW optimizer, which incorporates weight decay to regularize the parameters. A cosine annealing scheduler dynamically adjusts the learning rate during training, facilitating smoother convergence. The loss function used is CrossEntropyLoss, appropriate for multi-class classification tasks. The training pipeline involves efficient batch processing using PyTorch DataLoaders. During each epoch, the model undergoes a forward pass to compute predictions, followed by loss calculation and backpropagation to update weights. The validation process evaluates model performance without updating parameters, calculating accuracy and tracking misclassifications. Grad-CAM is implemented during validation to interpret the model’s predictions by highlighting the image regions most influential in the decision-making process.

Results

The initial performance of the model was evaluated to establish a baseline before implementing fine-tuning and hyperparameter optimization. This evaluation included an analysis of the confusion matrix, the training class distribution, and the training and validation loss curves over 10 epochs. These visualizations allowed me to assess the model’s ability to generalize and identify areas requiring improvement.

The confusion matrix at epoch 10 showb provides detailed insights into the model’s classification performance across the three categories: Healthy, Damaged, and Bleached. The model performed well on the Damaged category, correctly classifying 28 samples with minimal errors. However, there were notable misclassifications in the Healthy and Bleached categories. Eight samples labeled as Healthy were misclassified as Bleached, and four Bleached samples were misclassified as either Healthy or Damaged. This pattern suggests the model struggled to distinguish between Healthy and Bleached corals, likely due to overlapping features in these categories or limitations in the dataset. Improving this aspect will be a key focus moving forward.

The training and validation loss curves, depicted above, show the model’s learning progression over 10 epochs. The training loss decreased steadily throughout, indicating that the model was learning effectively from the training data. However, the validation loss initially dropped but began to plateau around epoch 4 and slightly increased toward epoch 9. This divergence between training and validation loss suggests the onset of overfitting, where the model performs well on the training set but struggles to generalize to unseen validation data. Addressing this issue will involve regularization techniques such as dropout or L2 regularization, as well as tuning the learning rate and other hyperparameters.
